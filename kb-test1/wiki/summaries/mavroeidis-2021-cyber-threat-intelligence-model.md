# Cyber Threat Intelligence Model: Taxonomies, Sharing Standards, and Ontologies

Mavroeidis and Bromander evaluate CTI taxonomies, sharing standards, and ontologies from the perspective of structured knowledge representation. The paper defines CTI as evidence-based knowledge about existing or emerging threats, including context, mechanisms, indicators, implications, and actionable advice. It argues that CTI becomes operationally useful at scale only when information can be represented in structured, machine-readable, expressive, and unambiguous forms.

The authors evaluate whether existing CTI resources cover the who, what, why, where, when, and how of threats and attacks, along with courses of action and technical indicators. They examine sharing standards such as STIX, TAXII, CybOX, OpenIOC, and MAEC, as well as ontologies and taxonomies from academic and practitioner sources. STIX is identified as a widely used expressive sharing standard, but the broader landscape is fragmented.

The main conclusion is that the community lacks a comprehensive CTI ontology. Existing efforts are often incomplete, non-interoperable, ambiguous, or insufficiently formalized for reasoning. The paper highlights conceptual ambiguity, lack of semantic axioms, poor integration, and weak support for inference as barriers to scalable CTI knowledge graphs and automated reasoning.
